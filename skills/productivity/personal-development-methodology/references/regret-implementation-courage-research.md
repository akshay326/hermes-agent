## 1. Regret Minimization as a Decision-Making Tool

**Core finding**: People regret inaction more than action over time (temporal asymmetry).

### Key Citations

- **Gilovich & Medvec (1994)**. "The temporal pattern to the experience of regret." *J Pers Soc Psychol*. ~1,600+ citations. Long-term regret dominated by inaction; short-term by action.
- **Roese & Summerville (2005)**. "What We Regret Most... and Why." *Pers Soc Psychol Bull*. DOI: 10.1177/0146167205274693. 362 citations. Greater perceived opportunity → more intense regret.
- **Nicolle et al. (2011)**. "A Regret-Induced Status Quo Bias." *J Neurosci*. DOI: 10.1523/Jneurosci.5615-10.2011. 93 citations. Status quo bias driven by anticipated regret; regret higher for commission errors than omission errors.
- **Zeelenberg & Pieters (2007)**. Regret regulation theory. *J Consumer Psychol*. People proactively manage regret through behavioral strategies.
- **Chorus (2010)**. "A New Model of Random Regret Minimization." 248 citations. Formal RRM model — people anticipate and minimize regret when choosing.
- **Bjälkebring et al. (2016)**. "Regulation of experienced and anticipated regret." *Emotion*. DOI: 10.1037/a0039861. Active emotional regulation mediates regret in daily decisions.
- **Liu & Liu (2025)**. "Can anticipated regret promote rationality?" *Front Psychol*. Anticipated regret improves decision quality and choice satisfaction.
- **Keinan & Bereby-Meyer (2012)**. "Leaving it to chance — Passive risk taking." *J Behav Dec Making*. 58 citations. Inaction is a form of risk; people underestimate risks of not acting (passive risk scale).

### Intervention Implications
- Prompt users to project forward 1-5 years: "What will I regret NOT doing?"
- Counter status quo bias by surfacing costs of inaction explicitly
- Regret asymmetry: frame "safe" choice (inaction) with long-term regret; "risky" choice (action) with short-term discomfort but long-term satisfaction

## 2. Implementation Intentions & the Intention-Action Gap

**Core finding**: 47.6% of intenders fail to act (Feil et al., 2023, N=29,600). If-then plans substantially close this gap.

### Key Citations

- **Gollwitzer & Sheeran (2025)**. "Psychology of Planning." *Annu Rev Psychol*, 76, 303-328. DOI: 10.1146/annurev-psych-021524-110536. Definitive 30-year review. If-then plans enhance attention control, memory, decision-making, emotion control, behavior change.
- **Sheeran & Gollwitzer (2024)**. "The when and how of planning." Meta-analysis of 642 tests. d=.27 for behavior change, d=.66 for affect regulation.
- **Feil, Fritsch & Rhodes (2023)**. "The intention-behaviour gap in physical activity." *Br J Sports Med*. DOI: 10.1136/bjsports-2022-106640. Meta-analysis (N=29,600): 47.6% gap rate. "Nearly at chance."
- **Rhodes, Cox & Sayar (2022)**. "What Predicts the Intention-Behavior Gap?" *Ann Behav Med*. DOI: 10.1093/abm/kaab044. Moderators: capability, opportunity, motivation.
- **Rhodes & de Bruijn (2013)**. "How big is the intention-behaviour gap?" *Br J Health Psychol*. DOI: 10.1111/bjhp.12032. Action control framework.
- **Saddawi-Konefka et al. (2016)**. "Changing Physician Behavior With Implementation Intentions." *Acad Med*. DOI: 10.1097/ACM.0000000000001172. "Strong commitment is only a modest predictor of goal attainment."
- **Sheeran, Webb & Gollwitzer (2004)**. "Interplay Between Goal Intentions and Implementation Intentions." *Pers Soc Psychol Bull*. DOI: 10.1177/0146167204271308. 602 citations. Implementation intentions sensitive to goal intention strength.
- **Armitage (2006)**. "Implementation intentions promote transitions between stages of change." *J Consult Clin Psychol*. DOI: 10.1037/0022-006X.74.1.141. RCT (N=554): sig. more people progressed through stages.
- **Bieleke, Keller & Gollwitzer (2020)**. "If-then planning." *Eur Rev Soc Psychol*. DOI: 10.1080/10463283.2020.1808936. 92 citations. 30-year review.
- **Wieber, Thürmer & Gollwitzer (2015)**. "Promoting the translation of intentions into action." *Front Hum Neurosci*. DOI: 10.3389/fnhum.2015.00395. 148 citations. Physiological correlates: action initiation becomes automatic.

### MCII / WOOP Protocol

- **Wang, Wang & Gai (2021)**. "Meta-Analysis of MCII on Goal Attainment." *Front Psychol*. DOI: 10.3389/fpsyg.2021.565202. 61 citations. 21 studies, N=15,907. MCII significantly enhances goal attainment.
- **Loy et al. (2016)**. "MCII Aligns Intentions and Behavior." *Front Psychol*. DOI: 10.3389/fpsyg.2016.00607. MCII narrowed intention-behavior gap for moderate-strong intenders.
- **Oettingen et al. (2015)**. "Self-regulation of time management: MCII." *Eur J Soc Psychol*. DOI: 10.1002/ejsp.2090. 128 citations. MCII improves time management.
- **Mutter et al. (2020)**. "Online RCT of MCII for smoking." *Psychol Health*. DOI: 10.1080/08870446.2019.1634200. Online delivery feasible.
- **Wittleder et al. (2019)**. "MCII Reduces Drinking." *Health Educ Behav*. DOI: 10.1177/1090198119826284.
- **Valshtein et al. (2020)**. "MCII to reduce bedtime procrastination." *Psychol Health*. Two RCTs.
- **Conner & Norman (2022)**. "Understanding the intention-behavior gap: intention strength." 448 citations. Intention strength moderates the intention-behavior relationship.

## 3. Commitment Devices & Overcoming Inaction

### Key Citations

- **Crockett et al. (2013)**. "Restricting temptations: neural mechanisms of precommitment." *Neuron*. DOI: 10.1016/j.neuron.2013.05.028. Precommitment reduces neural cost of self-control.
- **Soutschek et al. (2017)**. "Binding oneself to the mast." *Soc Cogn Affect Neurosci*. DOI: 10.1093/scan/nsw176. TDCS stimulation of frontopolar cortex enhanced precommitment.
- **Della Vigna & Malmendier (2006)**. "Paying Not to Go to the Gym." *Am Econ Rev*. 1,155 citations. People overestimate future self-control.
- **de Buisonjé et al. (2022)**. "Deposit Contract Financial Incentives for Physical Activity." *J Med Internet Res*. DOI: 10.2196/38339. RCT: deposit contracts increased PA via app.
- **Raio & Glimcher (2021)**. "Quantifying the subjective cost of self-control." *PNAS*. DOI: 10.1073/pnas.2018726118. Self-control has quantifiable subjective cost.
- **Ericson (2014)**. "On the Interaction of Memory and Procrastination." 14 citations. Present-bias + limited memory → systematic inaction.
- **Rozental & Carlbring (2014)**. "Understanding and Treating Procrastination." *Psychology*. DOI: 10.4236/psych.2014.513160. 239 citations. CBT most effective for procrastination.

### Intervention Implications
- Make commitments binding and concrete — even small commitments bridge the gap
- Reduce reliance on willpower — make action the default path
- Frame inaction as risk (passive risk concept — Keinan & Bereby-Meyer, 2012)
- Accommodate present bias: precommit in advance so immediate cost is deferred

## 4. Rejection Sensitivity — Mechanisms & Interventions

**Core finding**: RS is a well-defined construct (Downey & Feldman, 1996) with robust mental health consequences. No standalone RS treatment protocol exists; CBT + exposure for social anxiety (overlapping phenotype) is the evidence-based approach.

### Key Citations

- **Gao, Assink, Cipriani & Lin (2017)**. "Associations between RS and mental health outcomes: A meta-analytic review." *Clin Psychol Rev*. DOI: 10.1016/j.cpr.2017.08.007. 316 citations. RS robustly linked to depression, anxiety, BPD.
- **Gao, Assink, Liu, Chan & Ip (2019)**. "RS, Aggression, and Victimization: Meta-Analytic Review." *J Interpers Violence*. DOI: 10.1177/1524838019833005. 96 citations. RS predicts both aggression and victimization.
- **Foxhall et al. (2019)**. "RS and BPD: systematic review and meta-analysis." *J Clin Psychol*. DOI: 10.1111/bjc.12216. 104 citations. RS significantly elevated in BPD.
- **Zimmer-Gembeck & Nesdale (2012)**. "Anxious and Angry RS, Social Withdrawal, and Retribution." *J Pers*. DOI: 10.1111/j.1467-6494.2012.00792.x. 128 citations. Anxious RS → withdrawal; Angry RS → retribution.
- **Downey & Feldman (1996)**. "Rejection sensitivity: Toward an integrative model." *Psychol Bull*. ~2,000+ citations. Original construct definition.
- **Lord et al. (2022)**. "Construct validity of the Adult RS Questionnaire." *Psychol Ass*. DOI: 10.1037/pas0001168. A-RSQ validated.
- **Maiolatesi, Clark & Pachankis (2022)**. "RS across sex, sexual orientation, and age." *Psychol Ass*. DOI: 10.1037/pas0001109. RS elevated in sexual minorities (minority stress).
- **Wolitzky-Taylor & LeBeau (2023)**. "Recent advances in social anxiety treatment." *Faculty Reviews*. DOI: 10.12703/r/12-8. CBT + exposure strongest evidence for overlapping phenotype.
- **Leigh & Clark (2018)**. "Social Anxiety in Adolescents: Clark & Wells cognitive model." *Clin Psychol Rev*. 250 citations. Attentional bias, safety behaviors, negative self-imagery maintain social anxiety.
- **Sportel et al. (2013)**. "CBM vs CBT in Reducing Adolescent Social Anxiety: RCT." *PLOS ONE*. DOI: 10.1371/journal.pone.0064355. 126 citations. Both effective; CBT broader effects.
- **Beck & Haigh (2014)**. "Generic Cognitive Model." *Annu Rev Clin Psychol*. DOI: 10.1146/annurev-clinpsy-032813-153734. 1,010 citations. Maladaptive beliefs → biased processing → avoidance.

### Intervention Implications
- RS is a cognitive-affective bias cycle: expect rejection → hypervigilance → avoidance → confirmation
- CBT cognitive restructuring + exposure is the evidence-based approach (no standalone RS protocol)
- Address both pathways: anxious RS (exposure/activation) and angry RS (reappraisal/regulation)
- Implementation intentions directly counter automatic avoidance: "If I feel urge to avoid, then I take one small step"

## 5. Courage-Building Interventions

**Core finding**: Courage requires fear as a precondition (not absence of fear). Dual-process model provides intervention targets at each stage.

### Key Citations

- **Chowkase et al. (2024)**. "Dual-process model of courage." *Front Psychol*. DOI: 10.3389/fpsyg.2024.1376195. 13 citations. Process model: threat appraisal → fear → decision → action.
- **Gibbons et al. (2025)**. "Fear/Anxiety and Sleep Deprivation Combine to Predict Courage." *Behav Sci*. DOI: 10.3390/bs15050634. Fear is necessary for courage; moderate fear facilitates, extreme fear impairs.
- **Gibbons et al. (2026)**. "Courage Isn't Courage Without Fear." DOI: 10.20944/preprints202601.1431.v1. Definitional: courage requires fear.
- **Benson-Greenwald et al. (2026)**. "Mindfulness training reduces psychological threat and increases engagement." *PNAS*. DOI: 10.1073/pnas.2521857123. Mindfulness builds courage to engage.
- **Chiesi et al. (2022)**. "Positive personal resources during COVID-19: courage, resilience, mindfulness." *Support Care Cancer*. DOI: 10.1007/s00520-022-07123-1. Courage as positive psychology resource predicts lower distress.

### Intervention Implications
- Normalize fear as signal that courage is needed, not signal to stop
- Dual-process model entry points: (1) threat appraisal (cognitive restructuring), (2) fear regulation (mindfulness), (3) decision-to-act (if-then plans), (4) action execution (behavioral activation)
- Micro-commitments as graded courage training

---

## 6. Integrated Protocol (all five frameworks combined)

For each target behavior:
1. **WOOP** (Wish → Outcome → Obstacle → Plan) — creates implementation intention with mental contrasting
2. **Anticipated regret prompt** — "One year from now, what will I regret about NOT doing this?"
3. **If-then plan for fear response** — "If I feel the urge to avoid this, then I will take one small step toward it"
4. **Pre-commit to the action** — schedule it, make it public, create stakes (commitment device)
5. **Reframe fear** — "This fear means courage is needed, not that I should stop"
6. **Micro-commitment** — break action into smallest possible first step (reduces subjective cost of self-control)
